#Executive Summary:
Turkey occupies a structurally unique position as a NATO member maintaining diversified economic and diplomatic relations with both the United States and China. The intensifying US–China semiconductor rivalry presents Turkey with systemic risks concentrated in defense and industrial supply chains, alongside narrow but real opportunities in assembly/testing/packaging (ATP) and defense-critical design capability. A key finding of this assessment is that the EU Chips Act — often cited as a partnership avenue for Turkey — is now assessed by the EU's own auditors as very unlikely to reach its 2030 target, which materially changes the realistic value of EU alignment as a strategic option.

#Turkey's Current Semiconductor Landscape:
No domestic advanced semiconductor fabrication capability
High dependency on imported chips across defense, automotive, and consumer sectors
Defense industry systems (ASELSAN, ROKETSAN, Baykar) rely substantially on foreign semiconductor components
The 2020–2022 global semiconductor shortage significantly impacted Turkish automotive production, illustrating exposure to external supply shocks

#Strategic Risks for Turkey:

Defense Industry Exposure:
Advanced Turkish defense systems, including UAV platforms, depend on foreign semiconductor components. Export restrictions or supply chain disruptions could directly affect operational capacity and production timelines.

Economic Vulnerability:
Turkey's industrial base is highly chip-dependent. Any disruption in East Asian semiconductor supply chains — whether from Taiwan Strait tensions, export-control escalation, or natural/infrastructure shocks — would create rapid ripple effects in Turkish manufacturing and exports.

NATO Alignment Pressures:
Turkey's dual engagement with Western and Chinese technology ecosystems creates structural tension within NATO-aligned semiconductor security frameworks, particularly as allied export-control regimes tighten.
Strategic Opportunities for Turkey
Assembly and Packaging Integration (ATP)
Turkey could develop capabilities in semiconductor assembly, testing, and packaging — a comparatively lower capital and technical barrier entry point into global supply chains relative to wafer fabrication.

#EU Chips Act Alignment — Revised Outlook:
The EU Chips Act aims to double Europe's share of global chip production to 20% by 2030. However, the European Court of Auditors concluded in 2025 that this target is very unlikely to be met, with the EU's own projections now estimating only 11.7% market share by 2030 — roughly half the original goal. ASML's own analysis suggests reaching the original target would require capital expenditure nearly three times the Act's committed funding. This does not eliminate the opportunity for Turkey, but it reframes it: rather than positioning toward a rapidly expanding EU semiconductor bloc, Turkish engagement should be understood as aligning with a slower-moving, resource-constrained European effort that is itself reassessing its strategy (a Chips Act review and possible "Chips Act 2.0" is expected through 2026–2027).

#Domestic R&D Development:
Institutions such as ASELSAN and TÜBİTAK have initiated early-stage semiconductor design efforts. Scaling these programs could reduce long-term dependency in defense-critical applications, though design capability alone does not address Turkey's complete absence of fabrication capacity.

#Geopolitical Mediation Advantage:
Turkey's balanced diplomatic positioning may enable limited intermediary roles in technology supply chain discussions, potentially creating access advantages in certain procurement channels — though this advantage should be treated as narrow and context-dependent rather than a substitute for domestic capability.

#Assessment:
Turkey's most realistic near-term path is not fabrication entry but defense-critical risk reduction. Given that even the United States and EU — with vastly larger capital bases — are struggling to meaningfully reduce dependence on Taiwan-concentrated advanced manufacturing, Turkey's comparative advantage lies in narrower, achievable goals: ATP-stage integration, design-level sovereignty for defense-critical components (where the volumes are lower and the strategic payoff is highest), and diversified sourcing rather than domestic fabrication. The EU Chips Act's underperformance suggests Turkey should treat European alignment as one diversification channel among several rather than a primary strategy, and should weight bilateral or NATO-framework engagement comparably.
Confidence level: Moderate — landscape and risk factors are well-documented in open sources; opportunity assessments involve greater judgment and are more contestable
Policy Recommendations
Establish a national semiconductor strategy with defined 10-year targets, calibrated to realistic capability tiers (ATP and design, not fabrication, as the near-term focus)
Strengthen domestic chip design capabilities through academia–industry collaboration, prioritizing defense-critical applications
Engage with EU semiconductor initiatives and working groups, while tracking the EU's 2026–2027 Chips Act review for revised terms of engagement
Diversify semiconductor supply chain dependencies across allied and non-aligned vendors
Develop strategic reserves for critical defense semiconductor components

#Conclusion:
Semiconductors have evolved from an economic commodity into a core national security asset. For Turkey, this structural shift necessitates proactive industrial policy, supply chain diversification, and targeted capability development to mitigate external dependencies while leveraging geopolitical positioning. Turkey's strategy should be calibrated to its actual capability tier — design and assembly, not fabrication — and should not over-rely on EU frameworks whose own targets are increasingly described by EU auditors as unrealistic.

#Methodological Note:
This assessment draws exclusively on publicly available institutional reports, EU audit findings, and open-source industry reporting. No classified, proprietary, or non-public information was used. Opportunity-related judgments in this report reflect analytical reasoning rather than confirmed policy outcomes and should be read as indicative.
