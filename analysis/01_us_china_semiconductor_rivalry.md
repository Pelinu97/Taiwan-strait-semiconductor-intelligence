#Executive Summary:
The United States and China remain locked in an escalating contest over semiconductor design, manufacturing, and supply-chain control. Export controls, allied coordination, and Chinese state-led industrial policy continue to shape the trajectory of this rivalry. Open-source indicators through mid-2026 suggest that China has not closed the technology gap, but has meaningfully narrowed it through workaround engineering rather than direct replication of restricted capabilities. Taiwan's geopolitical exposure remains the most consequential single variable determining how this rivalry resolves.

#Overview:
The competition between the United States and China over semiconductor technology represents one of the most consequential dimensions of contemporary great-power competition. As foundational components of both digital infrastructure and advanced weapons systems, semiconductors have become a central domain of technological rivalry, economic leverage, and national security strategy.

#Background:
The global semiconductor supply chain is highly specialized and geographically concentrated. The United States retains leadership in chip design, electronic design automation (EDA) tools, and core intellectual property. Taiwan — primarily through TSMC — dominates advanced-node manufacturing. The Netherlands (ASML) holds a near-monopoly on extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography systems required for the most advanced chips. China remains the world's largest consumer of semiconductors but continues to depend on foreign technology for advanced manufacturing capability.

#US Policy Actions:
CHIPS and Science Act (2022)
$52 billion in federal funding for domestic semiconductor manufacturing, R&D, and workforce development
Major awards finalized in 2024–early 2025, including allocations to TSMC, Samsung, Intel, and Micron for US-based fabrication facilities
Export Controls (2022–2025)
Successive rounds of restrictions on advanced chips, chipmaking equipment, and related software exports to China, expanded incrementally since the initial October 2022 controls
Restrictions on ASML's ability to ship EUV and certain deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography systems to China, achieved through diplomatic pressure on the Dutch government rather than unilateral US authority
Entity List Restrictions
Continued and expanded restrictions on Chinese firms including Huawei and SMIC, limiting access to US-origin technology and tools
Multilateral Coordination ("Chip 4")
An informal coordination framework involving the US, Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan
This has not developed into a formal treaty-based alliance; coordination has been described by analysts as slow-moving, with members balancing China-related trade interests against US pressure

#China's Response:
Industrial Strategy
"Made in China 2025" targeted semiconductor self-sufficiency goals; independent assessments (e.g., Stimson Center analysis) suggest China fell well short of its original 70% self-sufficiency target
State Financing
Continued large-scale subsidies via the National Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund ("Big Fund"), including a reported third phase (~$47.5 billion) launched in 2024
Talent Acquisition
Continued recruitment of semiconductor engineers from Taiwan and South Korea, documented in industry and government reporting
Technological Development
Accelerated domestic investment in EDA tools, lithography R&D, and mature-node manufacturing equipment, with mixed and contested claims about progress on advanced nodes

#Key Tensions:
Taiwan Strait as a geopolitical and supply-chain pressure point
Accelerating technological bifurcation between US-aligned and China-centered semiconductor ecosystems
Allied government coordination (Netherlands, Japan) on export controls, alongside friction over economic costs to those countries' firms
Fragmentation risk in global supply chains as competing technology blocs emerge
Assessment
The available evidence supports the assessment that China is narrowing — though not closing — the technological gap with leading-edge semiconductor producers, and that Taiwan's geopolitical exposure remains the single most consequential variable in how this rivalry resolves.

#On China's Trajectory:
Export controls were designed around the assumption that EUV lithography is irreplaceable for advanced nodes. Open-source reporting indicates China has partially defeated this assumption rather than overcome the underlying restriction: SMIC and Huawei have reportedly repurposed older-generation deep ultraviolet (DUV) immersion lithography machines through multi-patterning techniques to produce near-frontier chips, with 7nm production demonstrated and 5nm-class development reportedly at approximately 20% yield. This is a workaround, not parity — yields, cost, and throughput remain inferior to EUV-based production — but it indicates the control regime is leaking rather than holding. Reporting also indicates a second firm, Hua Hong Group, has been preparing comparable 7nm capacity at a separate facility, suggesting this is becoming a broader domestic capability rather than a single-firm achievement. State financing continues to underwrite this push at scale, with a third national semiconductor fund reportedly in the tens of billions of dollars supporting advanced-node ambitions.

#On Taiwan as the Decisive Variable:
This progress matters less in isolation than in combination with Taiwan Strait risk. The US-aligned bloc's strategic position rests substantially on TSMC's near-monopoly over the most advanced commercial nodes — a monopoly geographically concentrated in a contested strait. If China continues to narrow the technology gap while Taiwan's security situation remains unresolved, the strategic cost of a future disruption to TSMC shifts on both sides: China becomes less dependent on imports it might lose access to, while global dependence on TSMC alone — currently a deterrent to coercive action — gradually decreases. Chinese self-sufficiency progress is therefore not a parallel track to the Taiwan question; it materially changes the strategic calculus surrounding Taiwan over time.

#Caveats and Confidence:
This assessment is a reasoned judgment based on open-source reporting, not a forecast
Yield and cost disadvantages for DUV-based advanced nodes are significant and may not scale economically
Figures on Chinese technological progress are contested in open sources; some originate from Chinese state-linked reporting and should be weighted accordingly
Confidence level: Moderate — based on convergent open-source reporting from multiple independent outlets, but lacking primary verification

#Methodological Note:
This assessment draws exclusively on publicly available policy documents, think-tank research, and open-source reporting. No classified, proprietary, or non-public information was used. Claims regarding China's domestic technological progress (e.g., yield rates, self-sufficiency percentages) remain contested in open sources and are presented as indicative rather than confirmed.
