Semiconductor Geopolitics in the Taiwan Strait

OSINT Intelligence Assessment: US–China Competition and Strategic Implications (with relevance to Turkey)

---

1. Executive Summary

This report assesses the geopolitical competition between the United States and China over semiconductor supply chains, with a focus on Taiwan’s central role in advanced chip manufacturing. Using open-source intelligence (OSINT) methods, the analysis identifies structural vulnerabilities in global semiconductor dependencies and evaluates second-order implications for mid-tier economies, including Turkey.

Key findings:

- Taiwan remains a critical single point of failure in advanced semiconductor production, particularly through TSMC’s dominance.
- US export control policies have accelerated structural fragmentation of global chip supply chains.
- China’s dependency on external semiconductor fabrication remains a strategic constraint.
- Turkey is indirectly exposed through its reliance on EU industrial supply chains and imported high-tech components.

---

2. Scope and Objectives

This analysis aims to:

- Map the structural dynamics of US–China semiconductor competition
- Assess the strategic role of Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC)
- Identify supply chain vulnerabilities and chokepoints
- Evaluate indirect geopolitical and economic exposure relevant to Turkey

---

3. Methodology (OSINT Framework)

This assessment is based exclusively on open-source material, including:

- Government publications (US, EU, PRC export control frameworks)
- Industry reports (TSMC disclosures, semiconductor market analysis)
- Think tank assessments (CSIS, RAND, Brookings, etc.)
- Academic literature on supply chain security
- Trade and macroeconomic datasets (import/export flows)
- Verified news reporting and technical white papers

Analytical approach:

- Supply chain mapping
- Dependency analysis
- Geopolitical risk assessment
- Scenario-based evaluation

---

4. Strategic Context: Semiconductor Supply Chain Structure

The global semiconductor ecosystem is highly specialized and geographically concentrated:

- Design: Dominated by US firms (e.g., EDA tools, chip architecture)
- Manufacturing: Concentrated in Taiwan (TSMC), South Korea (Samsung)
- Equipment: Dependent on Dutch, Japanese, and US suppliers
- Assembly & packaging: Distributed across East Asia

Key structural observation:

Advanced semiconductor production is not diversified; instead, it is functionally interdependent but geographically fragile.

---

5. Taiwan’s Role as a Strategic Chokepoint

TSMC holds a dominant position in advanced semiconductor fabrication:

- Controls majority share of advanced node (5nm and below) production
- Serves as primary manufacturer for US tech firms
- Embedded deeply in global defense, AI, and telecom supply chains

OSINT assessment:

Taiwan functions as a critical node with systemic global dependency, meaning disruption would have cascading effects across:

- Defense systems
- Artificial intelligence infrastructure
- Automotive and industrial manufacturing
- Consumer electronics markets

---

6. US–China Technological Competition

The semiconductor sector has become a central domain of strategic competition.

United States:

- Implements export controls on advanced chip technology to China
- Restricts access to semiconductor manufacturing equipment
- Encourages “friend-shoring” of chip production

China:

- Pursues domestic semiconductor self-sufficiency
- Invests heavily in indigenous chip fabrication capabilities
- Remains dependent on foreign lithography and advanced tooling

Assessment:

The competition is shifting from market-based globalization to strategic technological decoupling.

---

7. Emerging Structural Trends

OSINT analysis indicates the following trends:

- Gradual fragmentation of global semiconductor supply chains
- Increased state intervention in technology markets
- Expansion of “trusted supply chain” blocs
- Rising cost and complexity of semiconductor production

---

8. Implications for Turkey

Turkey is not a direct actor in semiconductor production but is exposed through indirect channels:

8.1 Industrial Dependency

- Reliance on imported electronic components for defense and manufacturing sectors
- Exposure to EU supply chain disruptions

8.2 Strategic Opportunity

- Potential development of niche capabilities in:
  - Electronics assembly
  - Testing and packaging services
  - Defense electronics integration

8.3 Risk Exposure

- Supply chain shocks in East Asia could impact Turkish manufacturing indirectly
- Increased global competition for access to advanced chips

---

9. Limitations

- All data is sourced from open-source materials and may contain reporting delays
- No classified or proprietary datasets were used
- Quantitative modeling is limited to publicly available indicators
- Geopolitical projections are scenario-based, not predictive

---

10. Conclusion

The semiconductor sector represents a structurally concentrated and geopolitically sensitive system. Taiwan’s role as a manufacturing hub creates systemic vulnerability in the global technology ecosystem. US–China competition is accelerating fragmentation of this system, with significant indirect consequences for third-party economies such as Turkey.

Future developments will likely be shaped by:

- Supply chain regionalization
- Export control escalation
- Strategic industrial policy expansion
